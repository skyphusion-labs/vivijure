Showcase: four films -- silent, scored, narrated, and now talking

Four real films rendered end to end on Vivijure, unedited renders straight off the pipeline. A silent picture, one scored with a music bed, one narrated with TTS, and a talking character lip-synced to its own dialogue -- across own-GPU Wan, Seedance cloud, and Kling cloud motion backends.

NEON HALFLIFE (silent, own-GPU Wan i2v)

1080p 10 shots ~30s own-GPU i2v silent

This clip is silent on purpose. Vivijure assembles a silent picture by default; scoring (a music bed, TTS narration, beat-synced cuts) is an opt-in Audio step you run after the picture locks. This is the picture straight off the pipeline, before any audio pass.

What makes it the proof and not just a demo: this was the first unattended full run, and it came out clean. Zero clips dropped (ten of ten shots rendered). It also recovered itself: the finish phase stalled partway through, the orchestrator re-adopted the in-flight work, and the film finished, all of it across a session restart with nobody watching. The system healing its own stall, unattended, is the part we are actually proud of.

FUR AND CIRCUITS (scored, Seedance cloud i2v)

1080p 8 shots ~55s Seedance cloud i2v scored (music bed)

The scored mode: the picture locks and the Audio step attaches a generated music bed, beat-synced to the edit. The music is generated, not licensed -- produced by the MiniMax Music module and staged to R2, then muxed into the final MP4. The whole pipeline, including scoring, ran unattended.

RUST (narrated, Kling cloud i2v)

1080p 3 shots ~30s Kling cloud i2v narrated (TTS)

The narrated mode: TTS reads the script over the cut, no music bed. Generated by the MiniMax Speech module directly from the storyboard text, staged to R2, and muxed into the final MP4. Narration is a drop-in alternative to the music bed in the same scoring chain.

Vivijure Speaks (own-GPU Wan i2v)

1080p 2 shots ~2.6s own-GPU i2v talking (lip-sync) CUDA upscale 2x

The talking mode: per shot, a generated line of dialogue is muxed into the clip, MuseTalk drives the character's mouth to match it, and a CUDA Real-ESRGAN pass upscales the result. It came out silent the first time, and a from-scratch re-fire then surfaced two more orchestration bugs before they could reach anyone; the writeup tells the honest story of the three fixes that gave it a voice.

Why Vivijure

Built for the creative homelabber, not a SaaS

Vivijure is for the person who already runs Proxmox at home and has a GPU sitting there: priced out of subscription AI video tools, and happy to own the stack instead of renting it. It is not a SaaS, and it never asks you to file a support ticket to change a sampler.

Bring your own GPU

Render on a V100, an H100, your own box, or a cloud motion API. Point the studio at whatever endpoint you have; mix GPU and cloud per shot.

Bring your own keys

Your provider keys, your accounts. The control plane runs free on Cloudflare; the expensive work hits endpoints you control and pay for directly.

Own every artifact

Keyframes, clips, LoRAs, and the final MP4 land in your own R2 bucket. You are never renting storage from us, and nothing is locked behind an account.

No subscription, no lock-in

No rent, no account wall, no per-second GPU bill paid to someone else. Self-hosted, AGPL, yours.

Module host, not a monolith

The core worker owns what is always true: project, storyboard, cast, bundle assembly, render orchestration, and a module registry. Every capability beyond that is an opt-in module worker plugged into the pipeline through a typed hook contract. Install only the modules you want; install none and you get a clean, empty studio.

Because each stage (plan, cast, keyframe, motion, finish, score) is a swappable module, you can trade an expensive cloud module for a free or local one: an Opus planner for a local model, a paid cloud motion model for your own GPU. Swapping a backend is a routing decision, not a rewrite.
